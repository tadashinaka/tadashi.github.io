Understanding Disasters: A Conceptual Framework

Group Coordinator: Tadashi Nakasu

April 28, 2025

Definition

Disasters are complex socioecological phenomena that emerge at the intersection of hazard agents and human social systems. They represent human—more-than-human interdependencies where physical events (e.g., earthquakes, cyclones, floods, droughts) and anthropogenic factors (e.g., industrial pollutants, toxic waste, contamination events) interact with vulnerability produced through historical socioeconomic processes.

Disasters are not merely external events that impact society, but rather expressions of unequal development trajectories, asymmetric power relations, and socially differentiated patterns of risk. From a world-ecology perspective, disasters are simultaneously producers and products of worldmaking processes—revealing how historical and ongoing political-economic structures shape human-nature relations across multiple scales, from the local to the regional and global, fundamentally affecting community well-being through disrupted value systems and regional ecological relationships that undermine sustainable co-existence.

Operationalize It

Applying our disaster research approach involves examining how disaster events and processes have historically shaped and been shaped by regional connectivity in East Asia. Just as World Ecology provides analytical focus on how capital, power, and resources/materials interlink to produce uneven impacts, disaster analysis reveals how these same forces create differentiated vulnerabilities to hazards. This approach helps us understand disasters not as temporary disruptions to “normal” development (building roads, factories, tourism, etc.), but as constitutive elements of world-making that manifest the contradictions within prevailing socio-economic systems.

Disasters function as boundary concepts connecting our research across scales (regional, national, local) and governance domains (economic, environmental, disaster). By viewing disasters as co-produced through natural processes and social structures, we decenter purely technocratic or naturalist accounts of disaster causation. Instead, we recognize disasters as relational phenomena embedded within broader systems that commodify and reorganize elements of nature. This perspective allows us to trace how disaster governance both reflects and reinforces existing political-economic structures, while identifying opportunities for transformation.

By examining historical and contemporary disasters through this lens, we can better understand the mutually constitutive relations between regional economic development, environmental change, and disaster risk. This enables us to move beyond siloed approaches to policy and governance, toward holistic frameworks that place ethical principles of justice and reciprocity at the center of social-ecological interactions.

Case Studies

Drawing on our disaster research approach (Forensic Investigation of Disaster (FORIN) with Development-Environment-Disaster (D-E-D) cycle model), each disaster case is as an empirical window onto the interlocking processes whereby economic expansion reshapes ecological systems and, in turn, re-materializes as risk. The project refines the model through extensive and intensive dialogue with colleagues working on regionalism, values, and community well-being within the broader World-Ecology Framework, ensuring that the analytical lens integrates trans-boundary political-economy, ethical commitments, and community-centered resilience.

Case studies through our approach with the project key categories:

Case / Key Categories D-E-D Interpretation (2–3 sentences)
Typhoon Isewan, Japan 1959
[R-Ec1,2; R-Ev1; R-D1]

Rapid post-war industrialization (Development) led to coastal subsidence and huge timber stockpiles at Nagoya port; imported logs sourced from Philippine deforestation (Environment) became lethal driftwood during storm-surge flooding, driving Japan’s highest typhoon death toll (Disaster) and triggering the 1961 Disaster Countermeasures Basic Act. This D-E-D cycle is continued with different actors and events.
Indian Ocean Tsunami, 2004
[R-D2,3]

[Thailand] Tourism-driven coastal conversion and widening social gaps (Development) removed mangrove protection and pushed migrant workers into high-risk zones (Environment); the tsunami’s impact therefore mapped closely onto class and ethnic fault-lines (Disaster), spurring debates on equitable coastal governance.

[International Disaster Governance] International Disaster Governance Mechanisms: Indian Ocean Tsunami Warning and Mitigation System (IOTWMS), Regional Integrated Multi-Hazard Early Warning System (RIMES), ASEAN Agreement on Disaster Management and Emergency Response (AADMER), ASEAN Committee on Disaster Management (ACDM), AHA Centre, ASEAN Disaster Management and Emergency Relief Fund (ADMER Fund).
Chao Phraya River Flood, Thailand 2011
[S-Ev1;R-Ec1,2;R-D2]

Industrial decentralization policies and foreign-investment incentives (Development) created dense factory estates on floodplains; altered river hydraulics and storage rules (Environment) converged with a long monsoon to inundate seven estates, fracturing global supply chains (Disaster).
Great East Japan Earthquake & Tsunami, Rikuzentakata 2011
[S-D1;R-D2]

Coastal urbanization and reliance on sea-walls (Development) combined with ageing demographics and eroded risk memory (Environment) to produce one of the highest municipal death rates when the tsunami overtopped defense (Disaster).

*Myanmar Cyclone Nargis case S-D2 including NbS will be examined and considered at FS stage.

Typhoon Isewan 1959 triggered Japan’s Basic Act and later fed into Asia-Pacific regional DRR norms, illustrating how altered development values and nascent regionalism can raise community well-being by institutionalizing risk governance. The 2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami sparked a new regionalism, seen in the creation of the IOTWMS and the AADMER. Guided by shared values of equity and ecosystem care, this cooperation puts community well‑being at the heart of post‑tsunami development, showing how value‑based regional action can break the cycle of disaster risk. During the Chao Phraya Flood of 2011, export-oriented land-use choices intensified hazard effects, while cascading supply-chain losses spurred regional BCP initiatives (APEC/ASEAN-partner programmes) on business-continuity, underscoring that balanced development values and livelihood-focused well-being represent cornerstones of regional resilience. The Great East Japan Earthquake and Tsunami of 2011 demonstrated how reliance on hard defenses combined with demographic shifts heightened mortality, yet culturally rooted recovery efforts, including pine-tree symbolism and festival revival, showcased community well-being as a resilience metric that now informs trans-Pacific regionalism and value-rich, inclusive risk governance.

Together these four cases demonstrate how research findings—enriched by World-Ecology’s attention to trans-scalar flows of capital, labour and nature—helps decode the structural roots of vulnerability while elevating regional values and community well-being as critical levers for transformative disaster governance.
